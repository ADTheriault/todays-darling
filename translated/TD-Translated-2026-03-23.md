## Title: I want to hold you.
## Date: 2026-03-23
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

I know it's a bit late to be bringing this up, but when the Beatles first appeared, that feeling of "What is this?!" is something I couldn't forget even if I tried. "I Want to Hold Your Hand" hit especially hard. In a book called *Shiawase na Ketsumatsu*, written by Kenta Hagiwara, Eiichi Ohtaki says this: "When I heard 'I Want to Hold Your Hand' on FEN, it was like a bolt of electricity shooting through me. This is it!" You can tell that even within Ohtaki's personal "great history of music," this song made an extraordinarily special impact.

But that aside, it's the title I want to talk about. In Japan, we all casually refer to it as "Dakishimetai," meaning "I want to hold you tight." I knew the original title, of course, and Ohtaki certainly did too. It's "I Want to Hold Your Hand." We'd all just accepted the Japanese title without question, but that's not really what the Beatles are saying in the lyrics, is it? The music and the performance are explosive, sure, but the actual content of the song is more like something a pure-hearted high school kid would say. "I want to hold your hand," they're saying. "There's something I want to tell you," they're saying. "I can't hide this feeling," they're saying. It's remarkably shy and tender, when you think about it.

That guitar intro crashes open like a thunderclap. It's right up there with the opening of Beethoven's Fifth as one of the most iconic opening notes in the history of the world. You throw the lid off with that massive sound, and then... "I want to hold your hand"? Doesn't that feel a little underwhelming? If that single's jacket had been printed back then with a title meaning "I Want to Hold Your Hand," it would have come across like some wholesome teen pop number.

So someone at Toshiba Records basically said, "Sure, they're talking about holding hands, but deep down they're crying out, 'I want to hold you tight!'" and that's how they presented it to the Japanese public. The reserved, bashful side of Japan actually punched right through the gentlemanly British restraint of the original. I think it was a great call, going with that title. Because, well, everyone wants to hold someone tight, don't they?

Thank you, as always, for coming to Hobonichi today. I wonder, did the phrase "I want to hold you tight" even exist in Japanese before that song came along?
