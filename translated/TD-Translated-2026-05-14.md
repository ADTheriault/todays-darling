## Title: On being a copywriter.
## Date: 2026-05-14
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

At a certain point, I quit my job as an advertising copywriter. It was always the kind of job where you only had to call yourself one, so saying I "quit" is a bit odd. What I really stopped doing was accepting assignments from clients. Back then, whenever someone labeled me "copywriter" in a bio or introduction, I'd say, if I thought of it, "I'm not really doing that anymore, so please just put 'Hobonichi representative.'" Eventually, correcting people every time became a hassle, and I figured if that's how they wanted to see me, I might as well let them. And before long, I came to think of what I'm doing now as more or less the work of a copywriter anyway, just on commission from ourselves.

The word "copywriter" used to be translated into Japanese as something like "advertising text-and-concept maker." The job, in other words, is to create "text and concept" for advertising. But of those two elements, "text" is the more visible one, and "concept" is harder to see in tangible form, so people tend to think of it as "someone who writes clever sentences." Back in the Edo or Meiji periods, advertising copy probably existed as one branch of popular literary composition. But times gradually changed, and while the output may still take the form of "text," the "concept" side grew far more important.

As "concept" expands and deepens further, what's really being called for is the concept in the fuller sense. The standard translation is something like "an abstract idea," but it's more like: "If you look at it this way, here's what you see." Concepts can hit or miss, of course. Say you're trying to find a concept for sumo wrestling: you might land on "an athletic organization expressed through the aesthetics of period drama." Not the most practical concept, I'll admit.

So if a copywriter's job is to think up concepts, then I've been doing copywriting for Hobonichi all along, on my own, without interruption. (Will this continue? Maybe.)

Thank you, as always, for coming to Hobonichi today. As for the "text" side of things, as opposed to the "concept" side, I think that requires its own kind of training entirely.
