## Title: You're already a little better.
## Date: 2026-07-17
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

I often think of what to write here for the next day while I'm in the bath. To put it a little more precisely, that's when some "little idea," the kind that makes me want to write something, tends to come to me. Today was the same. While I was soaking in the tub, I started thinking, "Come to think of it, I often hit on little ideas while I'm in the bath. Let me think about that a bit more and write about it."

This reminds me of something else. When you're at the hair salon getting your hair cut, you're really just sitting there doing nothing, and yet there's a sense of fulfillment, as if you've accomplished something. Your hair gets shaped up, and you come out "a little bit better." All I did was sit in front of a mirror, but I'd spent that time becoming "a little bit better."

With the bath, it's the same. You soak in the tub, let out a contented sigh, and just by savoring the moment you've taken care of the task of bathing. It looks like you're doing nothing, but the thing that needs doing is getting done. Here too, I'm spending time becoming "a little bit better," so beyond that, I'm free to do whatever I like. The premise is already in place, after all: I'm already a little bit better. That sense of ease is what gets my mind working better than usual. The "thinking" that doubles as my usual "getting a little better" task really does tend to turn out more interesting.

There are quite a few people who say ideas come to them while they're out strolling around, and this works the same way. First of all, they're "walking, moving forward," so they have the ease of knowing they can do anything on top of that. Just by moving forward, it feels "productive."

Now, for today's piece. If I were to wrap it up in some suitably respectable way, I suppose it would come to this: when you're tackling something "difficult," it helps to have something "easy" secured first.

Thank you for coming to Hobonichi again today. Yesterday I took up otedama, the traditional beanbag game. It's a pastime full of little discoveries.
