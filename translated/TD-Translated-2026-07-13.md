## Title: It's the sun we see every day, but...
## Date: 2026-07-13
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

Everyone knows about the sun. For us humans, isn't it the most famous thing there is? And why wouldn't it be? It's impossibly far away, yet we see its face every single day. Here in Japan, people call it "O-hisama," treating it with affection, with gratitude, even with reverence.

Thanks to the sun, or because of the sun, this and that keep happening, endlessly. Life itself was built on the energy the sun provides, and it's through our relationship with the sun that years and days exist at all. Since ancient times, no, perhaps even before human history, back in the days of our ape-like ancestors, the sun has surely been thought of as something special, an object of awe and worship.

These days we hear a lot about how long humanity can even remain on Earth, a growing sense of crisis about the future, and talk of whether or not we'll relocate to other planets. But there's a future far beyond even all of that.

Once you get this far, it becomes little more than a game played with numbers, but apparently, around five billion years from now, the hydrogen at the sun's core, the fuel for the nuclear fusion that makes it shine, will begin to run out, and the sun will swell dramatically into what's called a "red giant." It'll become a colossal star, roughly a hundred times its current size, and it will most likely swallow the Earth whole.

At that point, we can't exactly go on calling it "O-hisama" and taking it easy. Whether humanity flees or perishes hardly matters, because neither Earth nor Mars will be habitable at all. There's nothing to be done. True, it's so far off it's almost laughable. But the old saying that you shouldn't assume something will last forever applies to more than just your parents and your money. Looked at on a grand enough scale, even our eternity has its limits.

The sun formed, and then Earth formed after it, about 4.6 billion years ago. When roughly that same span of time has passed from now, both the sun as we know it and the Earth as we know it will meet their end. When you look up at the sun with even a little of that in mind, something almost like faith wells up inside you. After all, they say we humans are made of the very stuff of ancient stars.
