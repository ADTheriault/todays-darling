## Title: In praise of beanbags
## Date: 2026-07-19
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

Because I have my doubts about my own strength of character, let me get ahead of myself and offer up something that sounds like a comedy duo's stage name: "Easier Said Than Done." That said, honestly, more than I expected, this whole business of juggling beanbags is wonderful.

What I really want to master is the three-beanbag technique they call the "cascade," but rather than reaching for something that extravagant, even with one bag or two, once you simply start doing something, anything, you realize right away just how remarkable it is.

Off the top of my head, here are the benefits. One, your gaze lifts upward. Two, naturally, your field of vision widens. Three, the muscles and nerves in your arms come alive. Four, your fingertips get to work. Five, you exercise your left hand, which usually goes dull from disuse. Six, your shoulder blades start moving properly. That sort of thing comes to mind.

The day after practicing a little too enthusiastically, I actually had quite a bit of muscle soreness. It seems to have worked those so-called inner muscles too.

If you think a bit more deeply about the benefits, the fact that you're "using the predicting part of the brain" is probably significant as well. When you juggle, you're moving your hands by anticipating the next instant. You can tell this is true from how tired it makes your nerves, or your brain, or whatever it is.

Beyond that, the fact that it feels "pretty difficult, but like something I can actually do" is very convenient. If a "problem set" is like this, both beginners and veterans can play with it. That, I think, is why it's easy to keep practicing without giving up.

You can do it in almost any spare moment, in almost any place, and it has hardly any of the dangerous elements that come with sports. And there's no set order to thinking about it and trying it out.

Ordinarily, our lives are spent with heads bowed and backs hunched, sitting in a chair, staring fixedly at a narrow little display. We barely move anything except our eyes and fingertips. Juggling beanbags is probably the exact opposite of all that.

Incidentally, my own set of tools was five bags for under a thousand yen. So there you have it, my report on the all-around wonders of the humble beanbag. ♫

Then again, there's a version of me who can't stick with something even when it's obviously good for him. There's a not-insignificant chance I'll have completely forgotten about it a week from now.

Thank you for coming to Hobonichi today, too.

Juggling really is the finest hobby an ordinary person could ask for.
