## Title: What do you mean, "feelings of helplessness"?
## Date: 2026-03-11
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

2026 minus 2011 is, let's see, 15. So it's been 15 years already. Enough time has passed that I find myself doing the subtraction just to confirm it. Yesterday, a friend in Kesennuma said to me, "I thought we'd have to brace ourselves for this being a long battle, like maybe 10 years. But even that's come and gone, and it feels like we got here fast."

In the parking lot of the hotel where we're staying, there are no TV broadcast vans, of course. No crowds either. Walking through town, we remember "that moment, in this place" and say to each other, "Yeah, that's how it was." But this only happens because we're conscious of this special day, March 11th. Probably most of the time, the everyday just flows along here like anywhere else.

I think it's a very good thing that we've almost forgotten we once wished for these ordinary days to return.

For 15 years, I've come to Kesennuma every year on this day. No one has ever asked me, "What will you do this year?" It's always on the schedule. We only discuss how I'll make the trip. I come because I'm meant to see everyone in Kesennuma.

In the first year or two, after coming here, I'd be so depressed on the bullet train ride home. I thought the reason was "a sense of powerlessness." The sadness of "I can't do anything, I haven't done anything." I believed that was the cause of my low spirits. But really, who was I to even claim powerlessness? Looking back now, powerlessness was the starting point. Regardless of whether someone like me is powerless or mighty, here we are 15 years later, with everyone having reclaimed their everyday lives.

That was a "future" built by powerless people, each one of them, pooling their strength together, using their wisdom, using machines and tools, somehow creating it bit by bit. Even my powerlessness was part of those materials. That's how I can think of it now.

This might be the town where I have the most people I want to see. I'm happier than anything to meet everyone in good health.

Thank you for coming to Hobonichi again today. Not forgetting. That's the one promise I've kept for 15 years.
