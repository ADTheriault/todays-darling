## Title: Wishing over guessing.
## Date: 2026-03-30
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

Let me talk about this using a very simple example. Every year, at the start of spring, people do "pro baseball standings predictions." The Central League and the Pacific League each have six teams, and people try to predict what the final standings will look like when the season ends. These predictions are a tricky thing. The commentators, the experts, they make their predictions too, of course, but they tend to show a little favoritism toward the team they once played for. Fans who are complete amateurs also make predictions, but there's no avoiding the fact that their wishes creep in. They want their favorite team to win the championship, so they weigh all kinds of factors in ways that suit them, steering their predictions toward first place, or at least somewhere near the top. And honestly, it wouldn't be any fun if you didn't feel that way.

Then, about half a year later, autumn comes and the results are in. And here's the thing that really matters. When it comes to predictions, setting the professional commentators aside, having the result match your prediction exactly is not something that makes a fan happy at all. "Ha! Just like I predicted, my team finished in last place!" That means nothing. What are you even thinking, you idiot.

Now, this is about something completely different from pro baseball predictions, but right now, don't we have a mountain of genuine "what's going to happen?" questions no matter which way we turn? Experts and people who seem to be in the know are offering their analyses. Those of us who aren't experts are also thinking things through and doing our own research. We get pulled this way and that by all the predictions, the declarations, the loud voices, and both optimistic and pessimistic views come flooding in, so we wring out whatever brains we have and try to imagine how things might go.

But even if a prediction you didn't want to come true turns out to be right, it doesn't actually make you happy. When things turn out to be the worst possible outcome for a great many people, saying "See? I told you so" is nothing to boast about. And besides, the vast majority of predictions, including those from experts, are almost always wrong anyway.

What I really want to think about, in the end, is how we want things to be.

Thank you for coming to Hobonichi again today. You know, when it comes to all sorts of problems, the person who started the fire never seems to be the one who puts it out.
