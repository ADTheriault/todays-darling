## Title: Some days are restless. Calm down.
## Date: 2026-04-05
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

I finished a piece late at night, then killed it myself. So now I have to write a whole new one from scratch. These things happen when you're a grown-up, sweetheart.

I've said it publicly many times: I'm someone who finds writing difficult and doesn't enjoy it. Having to sit down and produce another piece from scratch at this point is just an unbearable hassle. Well then, you might say, why didn't you just keep the one you'd already written? Fair question, but the decision's already been made.

Why did I scrap it? I'd written it in fits and starts, with long breaks in between, and in trying too hard to stitch it all together into something coherent, it turned into something completely uninteresting. It's not like what I write is usually all that interesting either, but if I'm the one coming right out and saying "this isn't interesting," you know it must be pretty bad.

Right. Change of mood. I'll write this like a diary entry.

Yesterday there was a lecture by Masahiko Sato. The venue was the famous Yasuda Auditorium at the University of Tokyo. The title alone was already great: "Creating the Way You Create." It was a long event, two eighty-minute class sessions, but as you'd expect from someone who has devoted himself to the study of communication, Sato never let the audience's attention flag. He said everything he wanted to say, and the whole thing was engaging from start to finish.

And for anyone who came ready to absorb what he had to offer, I think it was genuinely, practically useful. A lecture that is both entertaining and useful. You could call it an ideal event.

Masahiko Sato has spent all this time researching these ideas, all this time refining his thinking, all this time nurturing other people, and on top of that he's managed to keep himself from getting bored with it. That's really something.

I was there as an even more attentive student than usual, breathing in everything Sato has been thinking about. "Doing interesting things, continuously, for a long time." I thought: there is nothing more wonderful than that.

Thank you for visiting Hobonichi again today. It's not great to have so many half-finished thoughts rattling around in your head, is it.
