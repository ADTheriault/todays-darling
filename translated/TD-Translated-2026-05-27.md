## Title: Sauce flavor.
## Date: 2026-05-27
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

When it came to deciding what to feed guests visiting from overseas, the standard answer used to be "sushi and tempura." The guests themselves had heard of these things, so they'd say, in halting Japanese, "I'd love to try that!" Nobody ever opened with "How about some natto?" But gradually, visitors started arriving better informed, and now things like ramen, onigiri, yakitori, and egg sandwiches have become quite popular too. Apparently, the quality of Japanese convenience store food was "discovered" during the Tokyo Olympics, held in the middle of the pandemic. And at some point, the rather formal phrase "guests from overseas" got replaced by the bouncier term "inbound," didn't it.

Every time news reaches us that some big Hollywood star was spotted eating ramen, it's funny how we find ourselves thinking, "Well, of course," feeling oddly proud even though we had nothing to do with making it. Lately I hear that tonkatsu restaurants are drawing plenty of inbound customers too. "Tonkatsu, naturally," I nod, as though I saw it coming.

Once things reach this point, you can't help wanting to guess what'll catch on next. I have a feeling it won't be natto. Tofu, made from the same soybeans, is already well known, and "edamame" has apparently entered the global vocabulary. But natto... that's going to be a tough sell.

Since predictions are futile, let me just go with personal preference. What I'd really like to champion is the vast universe of sauce flavor. Sauce already plays a supporting role alongside tonkatsu and the rest of the fried-food team, but I don't think it's getting nearly enough time in the spotlight. Yakisoba with sauce, not the Chinese-style kind, is wonderful, and the whole appeal of "konamon," those flour-based street foods, comes down to that sauce flavor, if you ask me.

So the next time you're eating out with a friend from abroad, casually steer them toward something with sauce. Don't worry about what good might come of it. Just do it.

Thanks for visiting Hobonichi again today. You know, the range of flavors and aromas in sauce is even more diverse than what you'll find in wine.
