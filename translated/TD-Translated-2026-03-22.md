## Title: Bringing more "oh, I see" moments to this world.
## Date: 2026-03-22
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

The serialized piece "Twilight," from the visit to Kuma-chan in Nara, has been a real pleasure. I was actually there in person, doing the talking, but enough time has passed now that I'm enjoying it purely as a reader. I hear the series will continue for thirty-plus installments, which makes me happy. We certainly talked enough to fill that many, I'll admit.

With Shinbo Minami (Shinbō), things have been easygoing ever since we first met. And of course they have. You can tell just by looking at him. But Katsuyuki Shinohara (Kuma-chan), on the other hand, is the kind of guy who looks like the toughest of the tough, even among rough characters. You'd think I must have been scared of him at first, but actually, not really. From the very beginning, he was naturally friendly with me. Here's a man who was always hanging around Shinjuku Golden Gai, where arguments and fistfights never stopped, stationed like a bodyguard for Jūrō Kara, who was himself famously wild. So sure, he was intimidating. I knew that was the role he played, but whenever I was up close with him, he was genuinely a good-natured person. I can say that now and people will probably understand, but back then, there really was a physical intensity about him that came from putting his body on the line.

What makes you feel at ease around Kuma-chan is a kind of tolerance, a way of just accepting things wholesale, in the best possible sense. Whether you're telling some trivial story or having a serious conversation, he simply takes it in with a casual "Oh, okay." And that "Oh, okay" of his is really something. First there's the "Oh," the moment of noticing. Then the "okay," a settling into acceptance, and that's that. And layered into it, in Katsuyuki Shinohara's own seasoning, is the hidden flavor of "Well, come to think of it, maybe it doesn't matter all that much."

I don't think I know anyone else who can say "Oh, okay" the way he does. If husbands and wives, parents and children, and if we're really wishing big, even strangers started using those words more often, I think world peace would come a step or two closer. Some people might find that kind of tolerance sloppy and hate it, but I think that's exactly where the "life-flavor" of being human resides.

Thank you for visiting Hobonichi again today. "Life-flavor." I just coined that one. Pretty good, if I do say so myself.
