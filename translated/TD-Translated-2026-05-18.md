## Title: A personal essay: the deliciousness of rice
## Date: 2026-05-18
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

I won't say every single one, but most sushi rice is delicious, wouldn't you agree? The seasoning of vinegar and a little sugar is part of why, of course, but the grains themselves are delicious too. Each individual grain is smooth and glossy, existing independently. You can really tell that rice is what it is: the seed of a grass, a plant called paddy.

Sushi rice is made by coating freshly cooked rice with seasoned vinegar. And what's crucial in this coating process is the fanning, with a flat fan or the like, to cool the rice down. You're lowering the temperature and driving off the steam. By doing this, the surface moisture is whisked away, and the grains stop getting waterlogged.

I know that was a rather tedious explanation, but I want you to understand this first. The opposite of sushi rice, where cooling and removing moisture makes it delicious, is what happens with "keep warm" mode. While cooked rice sits on the warmer, the steam has nowhere to escape. That steam keeps touching the surface of every grain. Naturally, the grains turn soft and soggy. That's the problem, right there. I want you to feel it. I certainly did.

There are all kinds of theories and methods when it comes to cooking rice. I've tried plenty of them myself, but lately, if I put it as simply as possible, I've arrived at this conclusion: "As long as you don't keep it on warm, it's going to taste pretty good." Transferring it to a wooden rice container is great. Even a non-wooden one works surprisingly well. Shaping it into rice balls is good too. Or after you've stirred the freshly cooked rice, you can just leave the lid off and drape a paper towel over it. You could even scoop it into a bowl and let it cool down that way.

The pleasure of "warm rice" belongs to the moment it's freshly cooked, and that moment only. If you're going to let time pass, skip the warmer and just eat it cold. If you want it hot again, the microwave is a better bet.

I realize you might be wondering what I'm getting so worked up about, but I ended up writing here the kind of thing I'd normally just say to a friend. Thank you, as always, for visiting Hobonichi today. Think about it: sushi and onigiri are delicious even when they're cold. Of course they are.
