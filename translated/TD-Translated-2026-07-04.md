## Title: Importing foreign culture and exporting culture abroad.
## Date: 2026-07-04
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

I think that's what I myself have been doing all along: living within Japan, crafting expressions meant to reach people who use the Japanese language, sharing a culture that anyone raised in Japan more or less understands. And it's not just me. I think there are many people like that. People have been talking about "internationalization" for ages, but even just the people inside Japan add up to a considerable number. In other words, Japan on its own is a reliable "market."

To the extent I've thought about other countries at all, I think it's been more about importing than exporting. Music, most of all, right? I never really listened to traditional Japanese folk music. Instead, we all shared in the fun-sounding music coming from abroad, and we loved it together. The same goes for art. Japanese-style painting was actually the rarity. We were educated in the history and aesthetics of Western art even at school, and the design all around us in the streets bore the influence of other countries. I mean, of course it did. We go through our days wearing Western clothes.

We imported foreign culture, absorbed it, and then expressed it within Japan's reasonably large "market." That's what we've been doing all along, isn't it? The audience was always, first and foremost, Japanese people. Music, painting, film, theater, manga, literature: almost all of it was fundamentally created with that in mind. You might say that creative expression has always been directed at people who already get it, people who share the same frame of reference.

And every now and then, some portion of that work would, to everyone's delight and surprise, turn out to resonate with people abroad too, and it would succeed as an export. Winning a foreign prize, or unexpectedly selling well somewhere overseas, that sort of thing.

But at some point, though I can't pinpoint exactly when, we began exporting to people in other parts of the world who speak different languages and were raised in different cultures. In some ways this happened naturally, in other ways quite deliberately. And I think there are things to be gained from this, and things to be lost. Both, without question. How are we going to live through this era where "both" is very much the case?

Thank you for visiting Hobonichi again today. It seems to me there are plenty of hints out there, even right now.
