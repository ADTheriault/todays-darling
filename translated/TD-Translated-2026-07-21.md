## Title: I still don't really understand.
## Date: 2026-07-21
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

I feel like I've always kept a certain distance from things labeled "love stories" or "romance films." A world of men and women falling in love, loving, parting, and weeping, that was never something I sought out to enjoy in novels or movies.

A long time ago, we happened to have the pamphlet for Zhang Yimou's film "The Road Home" lying around the house. I was admiring the cover, thinking how lovely it was, when I asked the boss of the household, "Was this any good?" and got a curt reply: "Not your kind of thing." That irritated me just enough to go watch it, and wasn't it wonderful? Oh, it was good, I cried and cried.

So even I can't quite make sense of myself. I absolutely love those old pop songs about falling for someone, being smitten, breaking up, weeping. And with dramas I've drifted into watching almost by accident, I find myself tearing up right along with the romantic bits, more often than I'd care to admit. Wait, do I actually like this stuff? Do I?

Lately it was "Everyone's Fighting Their Own Worthless Self." That twelfth episode, or was it, had romance running all through it, and there I was late at night, rooting hard for the lovers.

And then, though I can't quite recall what set it off, I watched a film called "We Made a Beautiful Bouquet." This one has an unusual shape to it too, but really, isn't it a beautifully made love story? I watched it closely, every minute of it.

Riding that momentum from "Bouquet," I ended up watching "The Moon on the Flatland" by the same director, Nobuhiro Doi. Sakai Masato is in it, and I had plenty of reasons to want to see it. Ah, and this one is like a romance that comes after romance, the kind of story that must be irresistible to anyone who has lived a fair stretch of life. Once again I watched it carefully, and let my tear ducts loosen.

I'm not keeping love stories at arm's length at all, am I. Maybe everyone loves a tale of one person being drawn to another. So why did I ever think I didn't much care for them? Now that I'm not going to the theater, perhaps romance films have simply become easier to watch?

Plenty remains unclear, but here's what I'm thinking now: maybe I'll read Goethe's "The Sorrows of Young Werther." Perhaps I'll pick it as the material for my "one-person book club."

Thank you, as always, for coming to Hobonichi today. When someone asks, I have never once said that I like love stories.
