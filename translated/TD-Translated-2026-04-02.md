## Title: Matsuko Deluxe on gourmet TV shows.
## Date: 2026-04-02
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

On so-called gourmet TV shows, there's an unspoken rule: you're not supposed to just say "delicious." The whole premise is that you convey something about the food without resorting to that word. So a kind of specialized vocabulary gets developed and deployed. Stock phrases of praise proliferate: "so tender," "nice sweetness," "there's a real depth of flavor beneath the spice," "just the right balance." But as these ready-made expressions get used over and over for convenience, viewers start tuning them out as meaningless. At this point, as a viewer, you almost just want the person to shout an honest, heartfelt "This is incredible!" You want them to convey how good it is through the equation of their own personality multiplied by genuine emotion. But very few TV personalities can actually pull that off with their own original expression. So they get by with those standardized phrases of praise.

The other day, though, I found myself genuinely impressed by Matsuko Deluxe. It was a moment on her show when she was given a muffin to eat. Matsuko typically announces beforehand what kind of thing she likes, what she's in the mood for. "Oh, I'm a sucker for this kind of thing," or "Hurry up, let me at it!" She sets the stage, then takes a deliberate bite, lets her facial expression and body language hint at the verdict, and then delivers the praise: "What even IS this?!" Something like that.

But with that muffin, there was almost no runway. She just put it in her mouth and sat there chewing with a serious, almost troubled look on her face. Come to think of it, I don't recall Matsuko ever expressing particular enthusiasm for baked goods like muffins. And it's the kind of food that saps all the moisture from your mouth. She just kept chewing, chewing, her expression stern the whole time. Even after swallowing, she still didn't speak. A tense silence. Then she drew a breath, and in a quiet, measured voice, almost like sharing a secret, she said: "This is incredibly good."

I felt a wave of relief, but honestly, as a viewer, I wasn't sure what to make of this reaction. Still, the little drama that had just unfolded was genuinely fascinating. This kind of artistry, this mode of expression, is something that neither a green young TV personality nor a seasoned veteran playing the role of refined food critic could ever imitate. Someone who is simultaneously director, actor, and critic is simply unbeatable.

Thanks for visiting Hobonichi again today. Come to think of it, has anyone ever written a proper critical study of Matsuko Deluxe? I don't think they have.
