## Title: Since I became a middle-aged woman, and beyond.
## Date: 2026-04-10
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

We tend to think of old men and old women as having always been old men and old women. But every one of them was once a bit of a troublemaker in their youth, or a bubbly young girl, and back then they were doing and thinking all sorts of things that seem unimaginable now, the kind of things you chalk up to being young and reckless. This just occurred to me, setting aside my own history for a moment. If you put it nicely, you might call it innocence, or tenderness of heart, and yes, those very same old men and old women apparently thought about such things too. They might wear a face that says none of that ever happened, but if you think about it with any calmness at all, it's obviously true that they weren't always old, right?

And the same goes for all those men and women in their thirties and forties putting on airs about being in the prime of their working lives. They were the new kid once, too. It's the same as a second-grader watching the new first-graders arrive and saying, with all the authority of a senior, "Aren't they cute? The first-graders are here!" It's funny, really. People just keep doing this, all their lives.

And the person themselves may or may not remember what they did or thought back then. There's plenty they can recall clearly, but there's a certain tone, a certain quality, that maybe they can't quite reproduce inside their own heads. The timbre of a particular age, its color and texture, I think those belong only to that moment in time.

The artless innocence of a small child fades away eventually. The fighting spirit of men in their prime is something distinct from any other age. And that knack old folks have for letting things float gently past, you can't really understand it until you reach that age yourself. Even within the same person, different tones emerge at different times. I wouldn't go so far as to say they become a different person, but I think they're wearing a different painting, a different piece of music.

Maybe that's why we can go on living without getting bored.

Thank you for visiting Hobonichi again today. It's been about thirty-four years since that song, "Even When I Become an Old Lady."
