## Title: Things that exist yet don't, things that don't yet do.
## Date: 2026-03-28
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

Once, someone asked me, "What do you want to become?" and I answered, "I want to become a home run." A few people read that, and some of them told me, "That was really good." It made me a little happy. A home run isn't made of atoms. It doesn't exist as a physical object. It's an "event" that has a name. And yet, plenty of people have witnessed one. There's the batter who hit it and the pitcher who gave it up. It definitely happened. It gets recorded in the stats. You can count them, one, two, three. You can even capture one in a photograph or on video. But if someone told you to set one down right here, that's an impossible request. Nobody possesses a home run. Sure, there's such a thing as a home run ball, and sure, there's the fact that someone hit it. But the home run itself? No one has it.

Thinking along those lines, love is the same way. Actually, it's even more complicated than a home run. There's no official record, and whether it even existed is, strictly speaking, unclear. But if two people believe it's there, then it's there. (In this case, just one of them believing probably isn't enough.) And even more than a home run, it can't exist as a physical object. Friendship, of course, is the same. Happiness, too. You can't pull it out and hold it up to the light. The best you can do is force some definition onto it and then try to reproduce something that fits that definition.

No matter how thoroughly you dissect a living thing, you'll never find "life" tucked away somewhere inside. But the fact of being alive is undeniably there. Dissect all you want, analyze all you want. You won't find life. Even though you can record the fact of living, and even though every one of us has encountered life firsthand. Come to think of it, life is a lot like a home run. You can build something similar with robots and such, but it's only ever something that resembles life.

Oh, and you know how we call music enjoyed in the moment a "live" performance? "Live" is, when you get right down to it, "life."
