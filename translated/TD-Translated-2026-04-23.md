## Title: Words picked up by *New York Magazine*.
## Date: 2026-04-23
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

The other day, I was interviewed by a media outlet called New York Magazine. They'd covered the Hobonichi Techo before, and I'd always been glad about that, but this time it was a direct interview, done remotely. The article based on our conversation in mid-April has already been published. The reporter turned out to be a longtime Hobonichi Techo user, and because they already knew the product so well, the piece had both a lightness and a depth to it that made me really happy.

After reading the whole thing, I noticed the title, printed in large letters: "We Designed the Hobonichi Techo," followed by the quote, "It's like a park for children where you have space to just play around and have fun with it each day."

That was something I'd said during the interview. I'd tossed it out casually, in a "how should I put this..." kind of way, just hoping they might understand what I meant. I've done plenty of interviews about the Hobonichi Techo here in Japan, but I don't think an expression like that has ever been used as the title before.

"It's like a park for children, where you have space to just play around and have fun with it each day." I did say that, yes. But the thing is, for someone wondering "What even is the Hobonichi Techo?", a line like that actually gets the idea across. Because people out there understood this feeling, that's why the Hobonichi Techo ended up being used by so many people in other countries. It hit me all over again.

More than listing out the merits in bullet points and emphasizing what a great product it is, saying "it's a place to have fun, like a park where kids play" does a better job of conveying what we're really about. Learning that gave me so much courage. If I could express all sorts of things even more freely, just as they come to me. I want to come up with good ideas more playfully than ever before.

Right about now, I'm over at Hobo no Eki AKAGI.

Thank you for visiting Hobonichi again today. And to those of you reading from far across the ocean, thank you.
