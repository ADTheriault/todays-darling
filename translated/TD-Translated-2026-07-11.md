## Title: What I'm thinking about on July 11th
## Date: 2026-07-11
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

I come to Kyoto around this time of year. It's become a kind of ritual, so here I am again. It has also come to feel like a signal that summer is beginning. For some reason, in this country, or perhaps just for me, summer is the season when I think most about the dead.

We remember the day a person was born. We call it a birthday. And then others remember the day that person died. That is called the anniversary of their death. It exists, I suppose, so that we won't forget that a life was lived. Every year, I find myself thinking, another new death-day has been added. A date that didn't exist while a person was alive is born the moment they die. Both birthdays and death-days remain until no one remembers them anymore, and once there's no one left to remember, they too disappear. You could say that every person carries two dates that are uniquely their own. As for me, I don't yet find myself wanting a death-day of my own, but I understand that one day I'll have one. "A good death-day," now there's a rather nice concept.

That reminds me, I got a letter from Kuma-chan, who passed away in April. No, he didn't come back to life to write it. Someone close to him, someone who was there on the day he died, sent me a letter that Kuma-chan had asked them to pass along. To put it simply, it read like an imagined conversation happening right then and there, something like, "Well, the time's finally come." When the moment truly arrived, when he thought, "So this is it," he must have decided, send that guy a letter. I felt grateful, receiving it.

We'd made plans for me to go see him around that time, but Kuma-chan's condition worsened and we had to postpone. Whether we'd managed to meet or not, I think it was a good death-day. I don't know whether everyone gets to have such a good death-day, but living with that intention seems like a fine thing. And come to think of it, even if a day was full of sorrow at the time, there are cases where you can remake it later into a good death-day.

Thank you for coming to Hobonichi today, too. Iwata-san, Sobu-chan, Kuma-chan, you may be dead, but you're still alive.
