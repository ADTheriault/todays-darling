## Title: I want to learn as if I were playing.
## Date: 2026-06-26
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

Learning something and playing at something are probably close to the same thing. Learning tennis, learning shogi, learning to swim, learning to dance. When the time spent learning feels enjoyable, it becomes play. But the moment learning becomes "for the sake of" something, it turns into obligation, and it's no longer play. It makes an enormous difference what the person doing it, the one at the center of it, thinks about what they're doing. You could say it all comes down to a difference in mindset. If you go in thinking, "I don't want to do this, but I have to," it will reliably become tedious.

Even when something is tedious, you can still improve, and I know there are times when you feel a sense of accomplishment and think, "I'm so glad, that makes me happy." But when there's a good teacher or a good mentor who can make learning feel enjoyable, the learner's mindset gets set to "this seems fun." Once that happens, the time itself becomes something you spend thinking "this is fun," and even a bit of hardship or difficulty becomes just a dash of spice within the play.

Learning from someone who teaches well, as though you're just playing together. I never thought such a thing was possible. And yet, it seems like this wonderful thing is happening in all sorts of learning situations these days. I grew up in an era when learning meant strictness, the occasional scolding, and the promise that mastery lay on the other side of suffering. That was just how it was, so I hated lessons. I'm incredibly envious of people who get to learn in a way that's enjoyable.

I imagine the theories and methods on the other side of learning, the "teaching" side, have been advancing too. Learning that feels like playing. Even now, even at this late stage, I'd love to try something like that. Age shouldn't matter.
