## Title: Remembering that I was once a child too.
## Date: 2026-05-23
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

Children think strange thoughts. And those strange thoughts, in their own way, end up nourishing something deep inside you later on. I might as well be honest about it now: I was genuinely alert to the possibility that a professional baseball scout, especially one from the Yomiuri Giants, might be watching me. I was particularly vigilant when I stood on the riverbank of the Tone River, throwing stones at the bridge pylons (ostensibly as pitching practice). When something "isn't entirely impossible, when the probability isn't zero," a child's conclusion is: it could happen.

I used to draw on hard-packed ground with wax crayons quite often. If you kept stubbornly filling in a large enough area, the surface of the earth would become slick and smooth, almost as if it had been waterproofed. One rainy day, I was doing just that when I made a "discovery": the rain didn't land on the slippery parts. I had no idea how to articulate this epoch-making law of nature, so I never told anyone. It's been about seventy years now, and I still haven't. Perhaps because I've kept this secret for so long, my thinking is occasionally infiltrated by a kind of inverted logic, along the lines of "a waterproof surface repels rain." Things like "bulletproof vests don't get hit by bullets." Grand, spectacularly wrong ideas. I consider it a personality trait.

Incidentally, I discovered you can buy wax crayons through a certain jungle-themed online retailer, so I ordered some right away. I'm thinking of giving them to my daughter's daughter, but she probably doesn't want them.

I loved books about inventions and was perpetually ready to invent something myself. In third grade, a classmate won a prize at an invention contest. It was obvious his father had made the entry. I was frustrated, but I couldn't say anything about it. Nor did I channel that frustration into working harder. And yet, for all that, I've remembered it ever since. Haven't forgotten it at all. Children, I think now, are the raw material of adults.

Thank you for coming to Hobonichi again today. The lies you told as a child, you remember those for a surprisingly long time, too.
