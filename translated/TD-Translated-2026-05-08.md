## Title: Is everyone doing well? Or not so much?
## Date: 2026-05-08
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

The term "May sickness" has been around for a long, long time. April brings the new school year, new jobs, the end of winter with temperatures climbing, all sorts of changes in your environment, and then, almost without warning (though it's not really a surprise), Golden Week arrives. When that string of holidays ends, you're thrown back into a daily life you haven't quite gotten used to yet. Kid or adult, it doesn't matter. Of course you're going to feel off your game. They say that all change, every kind, is stress. A new environment, difficult circumstances, sure, but apparently even things that make you happier than before count as stress. By that logic, even the beautiful clear skies of May are stressful.

When you put it all together, I have to say with a wry laugh: is there really anyone who's full of energy in May? What about you? Oh, me? I'm a mess, of course. I wouldn't call it "May sickness," but still. When I ask the people around me, just griping together, they all say, as if citing some universal law, "May is generally no good." I knew it, I think, and somehow we bond over our shared misery.

I can't speak for the Meiji era or the Edo period, but my feeling is that May in Japan has always been a time when people fall out of sorts. If that's the case, then surely, as a kind of survival wisdom, there must be some tradition or observance designed to help us get through this rough patch. Oh, wait. There is. The Boys' Festival, Tango no Sekku. Because illness tends to spread at the turn of the seasons, people would display warrior dolls to ward off evil spirits and hang carp streamers to swim in the wind, pouring their hopes into them.

Right. I'd been so fixated on the holidays themselves that I forgot to pay proper attention to the festival as a seasonal rite. Well, the best I can come up with at this point is that maybe I should eat more kashiwa-mochi.

Thank you, as always, for visiting Hobonichi today. This grumbly tone of mine might be the truest expression of my May.
