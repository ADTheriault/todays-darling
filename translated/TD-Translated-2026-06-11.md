## Title: Cowboys as workers.
## Date: 2026-06-11
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

Every day, thinking "What should I eat today?" Some people find that tedious, but really, it's a blessing. Just having choices is no small thing.

Years ago, there was an American TV series called *Rawhide*, back when Clint Eastwood was still a young up-and-comer. It was the story of cowboys driving cattle across the country, and every single day, day after day, their meal was the same: beans. I think it was salt pork and kidney beans stewed together. The cowboys would grumble, "Beans again? Every damn day," and then old Wishbone, the cook, would fire back with his usual line: "Don't like it, don't eat it!" I used to watch that exchange as a little kid and think it was hilarious. That bit, like a comedy routine, stuck with me all these years. I still think about it often, even as a grown man. What stayed with me is the hardship of eating the same thing every single day.

"Cowboy" literally means someone who drives cows. They were laborers, moving thousands of head of cattle for a living. Not the handsome types in ten-gallon hats twirling pistols and lassoing outlaws. These were men on horseback in rain and wind, driving herds day after day. And eating nothing but beans while they did it. People like us today couldn't handle that. It was brutal. The same went for the transcontinental railroad of that era. The relatively short history of the vast United States is, in many ways, a story of grueling, backbreaking pioneering.

I know this is starting to sound preachy, but being able to say "What should I eat today?" is something that rests on the mud-caked story of those people who drove cattle across the land and ate nothing but beans. Hey, are you listening? I'm talking to you, me.

I don't know. There's a kind of respect for that history that we shouldn't let ourselves forget. That's what I find myself thinking, even now, late as it is. No matter how much information piles up, no matter how advanced AI becomes, without a real, solid foundation underneath, it's all just clouds. Somebody has to provide the water, the beans, the cattle, the roads.

Thanks for coming to Hobonichi today, too. If America's history is one of frontier and pioneering, I wonder what kind of history Japan's would be.
