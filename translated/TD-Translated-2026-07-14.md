## Title: To you who have paused, a story about 300 monkeys.
## Date: 2026-07-14
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

People you meet as an adult, there are many whose birthplace and upbringing you never come to know. If you met them through work, you can imagine a certain amount about their past, but some encounters happen closer to pure chance. H.H. was one of those acquaintances.

It wasn't as though we were especially close, but he was the sort of engaging, likable person everyone warms to. Roughly once every couple of years he'd turn up with some rare coffee from who-knows-where as a gift and fill me in on how he'd been, though for the past several years I'd heard nothing. He was also unreasonably good at karaoke.

About a year ago, he got in touch after a long silence. "Monkeys are truly wonderful." That, delivered with total absorption, was the sum of everything he had come to tell me.

Deep in the mountains of a certain place in Japan that everyone would recognize, H.H. owns an enormous mountain cabin. A giant mountain cabin sounds like a contradiction in terms, but apparently he'd put the building up intending it as a factory, so its size had a certain logic to it. And yet it wasn't a factory, it was a mountain cabin.

The thing is, in that cabin, he says he's doing important work. He's a bit vague about what the work actually is, but he insists it's "nothing bad, nothing that runs afoul of the law or morality." He also says it "varies quite a lot depending on the time of year, the season, and economic conditions," but it seems to be a matter of concentrated, fine handwork.

"The local monkeys do all of it for me."

They don't live there, but some three hundred monkeys, he says, commute to H.H.'s cabin. They work diligently and industriously, eat their supper, and go home. The menu is something H.H. cooks up with all his heart in an enormous pot, and the monkeys, he claims, come to this "factory" because they want to eat it. Can this be true? Is he really the president of a company employing three hundred monkeys?

"For work that's simple but requires patience," he says, "monkeys are better suited to it than humans." He even showed me a photograph of monkeys peeling chestnuts inside the giant cabin.

Having written this tall tale, I'm off to bed for the night.

Thank you again for coming to Hobonichi today. It's a daily thing, after all, so I have to find ways to keep myself from getting bored.
