## Title: The lion chases the rabbit with all its might.
## Date: 2026-05-16
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

I'll begin with a quotation. This may be the first time I've ever done something like this. The source is Aozora Bunko, so you can read the full text there if you like. (I've put the furigana readings in parentheses and added a few line breaks.)

"In the autumn of the year 1902 of the Western calendar, on a forgotten day of a forgotten month, I hoisted a white flag from my bedroom window and sued for peace with the landlady of my boarding house. No sooner had I done so than the old woman set about hauling her one-hundred-and-sixty-pound frame up to the very top of the third floor. I say 'set about hauling' rather than 'hauled' in order to convey just how much effort was involved. The staircase comprised no fewer than forty-two steps. She paused to rest twice along the way. After an expenditure of three minutes and five seconds, the face of this magnificent woman, which ought to have worn an expression of triumph, appeared with agonized abruptness in my doorway. The surroundings shrank to nothing beside her. Addressing myself, who bore the honor of this audience upon my two narrow and burdened shoulders, the old woman, as the first article of our peace terms, issued the following command: Ride a bicycle."

I can't quite work out his system for punctuation, and the writing looks intimidating at first glance, but every line is plainly angling for a laugh. This is the opening of Natsume Soseki's essay "The Bicycle Diary." He blends a grandiose literary style with the patter of a comic storyteller, as if to say: savor my literary craftsmanship until you can't stand it anymore. And that tone carries on, unbroken, for the entire piece. What he's really writing about is simply this: he had to learn to ride a bicycle, and no matter what he tried it was hopeless and he hated every minute of it. Yet somehow the whole thing is rendered like the ornamental transom carving in a newly built house, executed with all the painstaking finesse of a master artisan.

After all, this is Natsume Soseki we're talking about. A great man. His face was on our money. A towering figure of Meiji literature. You might imagine he could dash off something like this with his left hand, scribbling it out like idle doodling, half asleep the whole time.

Yeah, no. I don't think so. He was writing in earnest, moment by moment, with all the seriousness that includes play and pleasure and devotion to craft.

Well, it's not as though I'm reflecting on my own shortcomings here or anything.

Thank you, as always, for coming to Hobonichi today. Soseki wrote this while studying in England at the age of thirty-five. Me? I can ride a bicycle just fine.
