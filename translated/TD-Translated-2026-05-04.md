## Title: An idle mind is the devil's workshop — just your typical long weekend.

## Date: 2026-05-04
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

During a long holiday, there's no time spent moving from one thing to the next, following a schedule, asking yourself "What's next again?" And that's a very good thing. Spending time in that unnameable, drifting way is actually what matters most. But at the same time, it also means you're prone to focusing on things you'd rather not think about.

Right now, having just said goodbye to a friend, my mind keeps turning in that direction. People say this often, but the death of someone close tends to show itself not so much as sadness but as loneliness. When you're buried in daily work, you don't have the luxury of sitting with that loneliness, and in a way, that's a mercy. But, though I can't blame the holidays exactly, having all this free time means I end up not feeling so great.

In that sense, the "Kayō Kissa" event at Shibuya Parco is a godsend. You go there, and the old pop songs fill the air, and all you have to do is let yourself be carried along by the music. Even if you look like you're just staring into space, you're not really idle. On May 4th it's my turn to do a talk, so I won't get to sit back and enjoy it from the audience, but I plan to enjoy it anyway. I'll be playing songs that are old even for someone my age, so the audience might want to brace themselves.

The truth is, if Eiichi Ohtaki were still around, he would have been the perfect person to talk with about all this. But he's another one who's gone to the other side.

This might seem abrupt, but I've been wondering: where and how is the word "believe" being used these days? Is it treated like some baseless emotion, something people dismiss with, "That's just what you choose to believe"? After all, it has nothing to do with so-called evidence or data. Our modern era has done a pretty thorough job of pushing religion, that part of human culture rooted in belief, into a far corner. I'd like to take another look at this old-fashioned word, "believe." Though it's almost too big a subject to know where to begin.

Thank you for visiting Hobonichi again today. The whole country is on holiday with idle hands and idle minds, so it might be a tricky time for everyone.
