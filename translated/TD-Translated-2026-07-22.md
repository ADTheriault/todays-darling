## Title: A match before the lord and sweet red bean soup.
## Date: 2026-07-22
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

In the old days, there were far more period dramas in manga and movies, so a kind of samurai era literacy took root even in children. Words like "blackened teeth," "vendetta," "masterless samurai," or that old saying about a samurai using a toothpick with pride even when he hasn't eaten, most grade schoolers probably knew roughly what they all meant.

Among all that familiar period drama knowledge was something called the "tournament before the lord." Swordmasters would fight matches in the presence of the shogun or a feudal lord. There'd be a well groomed samurai of decent rank, and a shabby masterless drifter smirking to himself, and someone wielding a strange weapon like a chained sickle. It seemed to be run as a bracket, with the strong ones fighting their way to the top. You'd win, show off your strength, and earn the lord's recognition. A chance at official service as a reward, honor, money. There must have been real reasons to fight in earnest.

In any case, it's fighting, but it isn't war. It's a "match" premised on being held before the eyes of "someone important." How you're seen, how you're judged, that becomes the whole point.

It's an event I only know from manga and movies, but when I watch the "debates within the media" of our current age, I can't help feeling that this is a gathering of "swordmasters" (self proclaimed or otherwise) holding a "tournament before the lord." The difference is that what used to be done before the "lord" is now done before the "masses."

They don't use real blades that would leave the opponent dead, but they swing wooden swords and bamboo practice swords and imitation blades with all their might. They let out battle cries too, and there are opening declarations, cheering sections, strategies, bluffs, and gambits. In the end, is being acknowledged as "you're strong!" what counts as the final victory?

Voices echoing "it is I," "no, it is I," and the more the arena fills with noise, the more the "lords" (the masses) multiply. In the old days they may have boasted of their skill with the sword, but these days, what strength is it exactly that they're all competing over?

Thank you again for coming to Hobonichi today. Tournaments before the lord are all well and good, but personally I'm more of a fan of sweet red bean soup served before me.
