## Title: On books, and on the act of conversation.
## Date: 2026-06-17
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

I buy a lot of books. Of the ones I buy, I read some of them fairly thoroughly. Others I just skim through the table of contents and the afterword, flip through the pages. And plenty of them sit in piles with a vague promise that I'll get to them eventually. The reason I buy more books than I could ever hope to read is a feeling that I probably should read them. They look interesting, or they seem like they might contain something I want to know. The reason I feel compelled to get my hands on each and every one is that books are relatively cheap compared to other hobbies and indulgences, and because I have this anxious need to be in the know. I'm getting on in years, and my energy and time are both limited, so there's really no need for me to know that many things or maintain that many interests. Even knowing all this, I keep buying books. I think it's because I have so many memories of being glad I read something, and because when something's nagging at me, I can look at my bookshelf and think, "It's in that book somewhere," and the worry eases a little. It's a sickness of modern life, surely. Maybe it's like some shady character living a dangerous existence who collects model guns. Probably not, actually. But still, I rely on books enormously.

And yet, I don't think books are what raised me. There are plenty of people in this world who were raised on books, but I'm not one of them. Books were certainly part of my diet, but the main course, the side dishes, the snacks, the raw material of who I am: that's people. Opening your heart and talking to someone, and having them do the same with you. That has taught me wisdom and ways of feeling tens of thousands of times greater than anything I ever absorbed from words on a page. Friends who were close by, teachers, people I happened to meet, and then all the mentors and companions I found after entering the working world. And yes, plenty of younger friends and colleagues too. The people who opened their hearts to me, who truly talked with me, they fill the vessel of who I am with an extraordinary richness. This is something I've done simply because I love it, and I've never stopped. That's probably why I still feel like I'm growing.

Thank you for visiting Hobonichi again today. I think the key, really, is that part about opening your heart.
