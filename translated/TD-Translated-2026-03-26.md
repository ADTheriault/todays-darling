## Title: The past was once merely the present.
## Date: 2026-03-26
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

Over the years, I've had the chance to meet all sorts of people older than me, and I'd listen to them talk about the "old days" they'd lived through. So many times I'd think: ah, people from later generations see it one way, but those who were actually there at the time saw something completely different. I always found these stories fascinating, precious even.

But somewhere along the way, I became the one being asked. And of course I did. I have firsthand knowledge of things from more than half a century ago. (Even someone born in the Showa era can say that sixty years before their birth was still the Edo period.) That's how much time has passed.

Yesterday there was news that Seibu Shibuya is closing. It opened in 1968, and I've known it since around then. I worked on advertising campaigns there, took part in all kinds of events. The things I saw and know are themselves "the old days" now. So if I just talk casually about Seibu Shibuya, younger people probably hear it as a tale from long ago. This sort of thing has been happening more and more.

When people react with "Wow, is that really how it was?!" or lean in and say "Tell me more," I can never quite feel proud about it. Why is that?

Probably because the person listening and I are both living in the same present, the year 2026. At the very least, I believe I'm breathing in this era the same way you are, living through the same moment you are. On this point, I'm fairly confident. More so than plenty of people younger than me, I am living in the present. It's not that I want to seem young. I genuinely believe it's true.

When I do talk about the past, I'm talking about it the same way I'd talk about the present. Not "things were better back then." Just: this is how it was. The present, too, will one day be someone's past. And the past, when it was happening, was simply the present. What changes is the years piling up. That said, even now, I remain as immature as ever.

Listen, everyone. The things you're experiencing now, the things you're feeling right now, will someday be told as stories of the old days.

Thank you for coming to Hobonichi today. Being alive means making memories. And today, we're making them still.
