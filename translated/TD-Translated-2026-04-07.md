## Title: An old man placing a large rock in the middle of the road.
## Date: 2026-04-07
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

In what must have been very near the end of his life, Takaaki Yoshimoto said something to me in a rather forceful tone: "These days, everything is about communication, right? So I've been thinking I'd like to take a big boulder and drop it right in the middle of all that communication. Just to get in the way."

He was completely serious, so I took it at face value. I accepted what he said, though I didn't really understand it. Communication is a kind of traffic, so I pictured something like a road. And there in the middle of that road, where information flows back and forth, was old Mr. Yoshimoto, drenched in sweat, heaving an enormous stone into place to block the way. Like something out of a cartoon. That image came to mind, and just that was already funny. But as for why he felt compelled to do such a thing, well, it couldn't have been mere contrarianism. Still, sitting there listening to him at the time, all I felt was that it was amusing.

Yet somehow those words left a deep impression. The image of "dropping a huge stone in the middle of communication" stayed with me. It was like a poster pinned up inside my head that never came down.

Lately, though, even if I still can't put it well, I feel like I'm starting to understand it more. When "communication" is valued too highly, what develops is efficiency in getting your point across, techniques for organizing your message neatly, methods for transmitting more and more. And now, looking around, that era has truly arrived. The current obsession with "putting things into words" is part of it. The rise of AI is pushing things further in that direction. The excess flesh of language, its blemishes, its wrinkles, all being stripped away. Just as faces and bodies are reshaped by cosmetic surgery, language, too, is being steered toward some "ideal" of easy comprehension.

And onto that heavily trafficked road, Yoshimoto drops his big stone. That stone could be, for instance, something like poetry.

Thank you, as always, for coming to Hobonichi today. What I'm saying here may not come across so well either.
