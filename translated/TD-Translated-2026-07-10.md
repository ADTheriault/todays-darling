## Title: Singing in front of about a hundred people.
## Date: 2026-07-10
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

Take Akiko Yano, for example. If you asked whether she'd been performing at Orchard Hall since way back, the answer would be not at all. She was doing live shows at Janjan in Shibuya, perfectly ordinary ones. Packing in fewer than a hundred people, crammed tight together. Tatsuro Yamashita was playing at a live house in, I think it was Koenji. That was fewer than a hundred people too, of course. When RC Succession relaunched with that style of theirs, they were playing a live house in Shibuya that held fewer than a hundred. "If every one of you here brought just one more friend, we'd double this audience!" Cleary was how he put it, very concrete, very specific.

Whether someone was famous, whether they were popular or not, the difference probably only amounted to whether you needed a numbered ticket to get in. Every artist was well known to the people who loved them, but filling a large venue on your own wasn't something that happened easily. If you traced the timeline, sure, this song came out in this year, the lineup changed here, it's all there in the record, and the fans know it. But none of it was really creating some massive wave.

Eiichi Ohtaki kept putting out album after album from his studio in Fussa, but the only people buying them were the fans who were eagerly waiting for each one. I realize now, belatedly, that there weren't actually all that many of them. A hundred people, two hundred, a thousand at most. Music was being written and performed in front of numbers you could see, numbers you could count. What you might call handcrafted music was simply the norm.

That got multiplied and scaled up, and we arrived at this era where mega-hits are the thing everyone talks about. But even today's massive hitmakers must have started out standing in front of a hundred people. At the starting line, everyone's the same.

Thanks for coming to Hobonichi today, too. What matters is being able to sing joyfully in front of a small audience.
