## Title: When there's only one option to choose from.
## Date: 2026-04-09
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

I had a conversation today, and this is something that came up during it. You know that scene you see in dramas all the time, where a man goes to meet his partner's father to ask for permission to marry? When the father asks something like, "Do you promise to love my daughter for the rest of your life?" there is only one possible answer. "Well, who can say what the future holds" or "I think the nature of love changes over time" or "I probably intend to, yes," any of those would be absolutely unacceptable. The shortest acceptable response is simply an immediate, unhesitating "Yes." If you try to come up with what you think is the "right" answer, every single one of those attempts gets sucked straight into a black hole of wrong answers. If you pause for more than three seconds, or in the worst case more than ten, then even if you do finally say yes, you've already been disqualified and shown the door. This is not a moment for earnest deliberation. And if you're the argumentative type who purses his lips and protests, "But I want to tell the truth! I want to face this with genuine sincerity!" well, at that point you're getting permanently banned from the premises.

The world is full of questions like this, questions that have no real choice in them. In the world of sports, or anywhere that winning and losing exist, I think the only thing you can say is "I will absolutely win." There's really nothing else, is there? In work where results aren't measured in wins and losses, you can sometimes say, "It's okay to fail. Don't be afraid!" But in any arena where there's a clear outcome, a winner and a loser, I've never once heard someone say, "It's okay to lose."

Which makes me wonder: if you actually went in thinking "it's okay to lose," would you really end up further from winning? It's true that continuing to push forward with the intent to win is part of the etiquette of competition. And yes, that mindset probably does give you a better chance of winning. But is it really, truly impossible that a bold, liberated attempt born from the thought "hey, it's okay if I lose" could ever work? The possibility of losing is always there, obviously. And maybe, just maybe, if someone sent you off with a laugh and the words "It's fine if you lose," you'd feel lighter, freer, and actually play better. I think that could happen sometimes, at least.

Thank you for coming to Hobonichi today. To be perfectly honest, I don't think I could say anything other than "I'll win" either.
