## Title: Even if a mistake is still a mistake.
## Date: 2026-04-11
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

There's a kind of writing that's trying to make you laugh. Sometimes you can tell right away, and sometimes you can't. I like the kind that makes you laugh the moment you read it, but I also like the kind where the humor dawns on you after a beat. I'm fond of both. If I could, I'd read nothing but writing like that. Then again, maybe a steady diet of it would be too much. Writing can't be all about making people laugh.

So then, I try flipping the question around. What kind of writing do I not want to read? Well, you can't really know whether you want to read something until you've read it, so that's a dead end. Let me rephrase: what kind of writing am I bad with?

(Here I spent a few minutes thinking it over.)

Even when it doesn't say so directly, writing that's essentially telling you to hate something or someone. That's the kind I have trouble with. It's perfectly fine to say "that's wrong" about something. Of course there are people who think differently from you, and you're free to say "I disagree." But when you're convinced you're right, it becomes easy to spot the "wrongness" in those who see things differently, and then to leap from "wrong" to "wrong equals evil." That's where the momentum carries you too far. Before you know it, "wrong" equals "evil" equals "enemy." Once there's heat behind it, you start wanting allies for your position. And then you end up issuing orders, or instructions, to everyone within earshot: "Hate them!" This is something even national leaders have done time and again. What people describe as "stoking division" is, more often than not, a "Hate them!" message at its core.

From international politics down to neighborhood disputes, so much of what gets put out into the world is built on the theme of "Hate!" No matter how well-meaning the person saying it may be, I have a hard time with "Hate!" Something can be wrong, and I'll accept it's wrong, but "Hate!" is where I struggle.

Thank you, as always, for coming to Hobonichi today. After all, the possibility that I'm the one who's wrong is always there.
