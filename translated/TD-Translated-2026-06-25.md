## Title: What should you do with your lives?
## Date: 2026-06-25
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

If a young person asked me, "What should I do with my life?" would I be able to answer? I've been turning this over in my mind for a while now. Come to think of it, I never asked anyone that question myself. Back then, all I was really thinking about was whether I could survive. Even the idea of "finding happiness" felt like an aspiration that didn't suit someone like me. And yet, strangely, I wasn't much of a worrier in those days.

But setting myself aside. When a young person wants to know, "What should I be doing?" I can understand that feeling. It probably comes from a place of, "If I just knew what it was, I'd be ready to work hard." Like saying, "Give me the problem, and I'll do everything I can to solve it."

The truth is, finding the problem is itself the problem, and the answer to that problem is simply discovering what problem you need to solve. But that's a question that spans an entire lifetime, so saying as much doesn't really help.

I want to think about this for my own sake, too. What should young people be doing?

When I search my memory, the mentors in my life all said pretty much the same thing. "Read the classics." It may have been a kind of stock answer. There was also, "Get your heart broken, many times over." You could say these are harmless, inoffensive pieces of advice.

But compared to the sort of guidance that came along in a more recent era, things like "Get an MBA" or "Build your network" or "Study computers," advice that sounds so eminently practical, I don't think those older words are the least bit inferior. "Read the classics" and "Get your heart broken" both seem to be ways of saying, "Try to understand what it means to be human."

Well, I'll keep thinking about it.

Thank you for visiting Hobonichi again today. "What should I be doing?" It's a question that grown-ups and the elderly are still asking, too.
