## Title: Is the other person's face really my mirror?
## Date: 2026-04-15
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

Someone with a great deal of life experience once said, "If you watch the other person's face, you can tell what your own face looks like. When they seem to be in a bad mood, you have to assume your face is doing the same thing." I thought, yeah, that makes sense. It probably depends on the situation, but you can't go wrong thinking that way.

It reminded me of an old joke. There was a monkey in a box, the kind that imitates everything you do. The box had a hole in it, so someone decided to peek inside and see what the monkey was up to. But they couldn't see a thing. The monkey was peeking back at them from the other side.

That sort of thing really does happen all the time, I was thinking. When you're driving a car at fifty kilometers an hour, it also means the scenery in front of you is rushing toward you at fifty kilometers an hour. There's something a little frightening about that.

Come to think of it, there's a parable like this too. A traveler arrived at a village and asked one of the villagers what kind of place it was. The villager turned the question around: what was the last village you stayed in like? The traveler said everyone there had been unkind, ill-tempered, the worst village imaginable. The villager nodded knowingly and said, well then, this village is probably that kind of village too.

I also recall something Takaaki Yoshimoto once said. "When we act upon nature and change it, nature is simultaneously acting upon us and changing us." Where there is action, there is an equal reaction.

This side and that side, humans and nature, the other person and yourself. When you set two things face to face, you get action and reaction. But in reality, there are other ways to talk about it: the two things "merge into one," or "the distance between them grows," or "you redraw the lines entirely." Depending on how you frame the relationship, the story changes.
