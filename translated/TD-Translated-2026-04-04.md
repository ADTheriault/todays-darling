## Title: I quit my smartphone addiction just like that.
## Date: 2026-04-04
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

Yesterday, I wrote here about how the "magic box that holds everything," the smartphone, is just so incredible that I can't let go of it. I said I really need to think about pulling back a little from this state of total reliance, this dependency. Not that I'd throw away this extraordinary "magic box," but I want to develop a healthy sense of distance, so it doesn't become a dependency. How do you even figure out what the right "distance" is?

On the very day that piece was published on Hobonichi, it seems the communication functions on my iPhone broke down, and suddenly I couldn't make any contact while I was out and about. I was stunned. I had no idea what exactly wasn't working, so I tried all sorts of things. I figured out that no new information was coming in, and I couldn't send anything out either. The screen was perfectly visible and readable, but having no information update at all... that's a problem. Could I still use my transit card? What about my digital tickets? (Going through the whole ordeal in detail would be tedious, so I'll skip right ahead: it's fixed now.)

The thing is, when I was half-ironically talking about being dependent on my smartphone, it never occurred to me that it might just break. When I was imagining things like locking it in a safe for a while to get some distance, there was always an underlying sense of security: when the time was up, I could use it again. When I was making plans to take breaks from it, to use it in moderation so I wouldn't be too dependent, I was assuming all along that it would always be there whenever I wanted it.

There's a saying: "Don't assume your parents and your money will always be there." Well, the same goes for oil and smartphones, doesn't it? When it comes to this "magic box," it's not just the battery dying. There were always countless "chances for it to stop working." Losing it somewhere, dropping it and having a car run over it.

I really am going to think this over again. The right distance. Like kids making promises with their parents about screen time, maybe I'll set my own voluntary limits, so many hours a day. It might be a bit like cutting back on overeating and stopping at eighty percent full.

Thank you for coming to Hobonichi today. But, ha, don't stop coming to Hobonichi, okay?
