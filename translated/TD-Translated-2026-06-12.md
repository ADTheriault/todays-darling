## Title: I had ramen again today, didn't I.
## Date: 2026-06-12
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

I'm a pretty big fan of ramen. First of all, I enjoy ramen as information. If there's an article about ramen, I'll read it. If the opportunity comes up, I'll visit shops here and there to eat. I have my favorites that I return to, of course, but if some new place catches my attention, I'll make the trip. I watch that BS-TBS show called "Eating Ramen." I have friends who are far more serious about ramen than I am, and I enjoy listening to them talk about it. I love eating the ramen itself, but learning about that whole world is fascinating too.

I think ramen fans, myself included, tend to look at the current ramen industry the way you'd watch a competitive sport. The playing field is fixed: one bowl. The time it takes to eat, the price per customer, the general ingredients, all more or less set. Noodles, broth, toppings. Those are the elements you compete with. You can put in as much effort as you want, source your ingredients as carefully as you like, go as far as your freedom takes you. If someone wants to pour their life into it, that's fine. What matters is whether the resulting bowl wins over the customer. Value equals victory, and the results show up in the lines, the reputation, the sales.

As knowledge and information about ramen accumulate, you get more and more so-called connoisseurs with an unreasonable depth of expertise. That's another way it resembles competitive sports. Sometimes a customer ends up opening their own ramen shop. Just as strategy and tactics in sports become increasingly specialized, the methods for making broth grow into something like arcane secret arts. Some of it can be understood through logic and science. Some of it sounds more like the work of an alchemist. Either way is fine, as long as it produces something of value. Ramen as reading material is thrilling and fascinating.

All of that is how I genuinely feel, but then again... a simple "ramen" sitting on the menu at a good neighborhood Chinese joint, that's delicious too. Something that doesn't seem to involve any secret techniques at all. If it tastes good, it wins. Being just a regular ramen-loving customer is a pretty carefree deal. Sorry about that.

Thanks for visiting Hobonichi again today. I'm probably on the not-very-knowledgeable end of the ramen-lover spectrum.
