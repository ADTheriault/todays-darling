## Title: Things done, things not done, and things left half-done.
## Date: 2026-05-13
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

When someone has seen something through to the end, or kept at something for a long time, we tend to say that's really something. I have things like that myself. This daily column for Hobonichi, for instance, I've kept going without a break for close to twenty-eight years now.

And then there must be plenty of things I've never done at all. Well, of course there are. Most things, I haven't done. To use my own example: I've never taken up golf. Once, just once, a friend invited me out to the course. Afterward, I never had the slightest feeling I'd do it again. I was invited on two separate occasions, and even received a set of clubs as a gift. But in the end, I never played. Mahjong was the same. I'd be sitting in the room while friends played, with no desire whatsoever to join in, just reading a book. I've played a handful of times over the course of my life, but I have no fond memories of it, and no urge to play again.

Golf and mahjong are just the obvious examples. The things I don't do are essentially infinite. Among them are things I've deliberately decided not to do.

But beyond doing and not doing, there's a third category. Things I started but couldn't keep up. In my case, there are actually a great many of these.

Let me dredge up some you probably haven't heard about. Take the shamisen, for instance. I got it into my head that I wanted to play the musical introductions for rakugo performances myself. So I bought one in Kyoto, found a teacher, and got ready to begin lessons. After just one session, I was done. Hopeless. Impossible. On days the teacher was supposed to come, I'd slip out the back door. Instruments in general have been a total loss, and anything involving exercise lasted two sessions at most. Language study, lessons of any kind, the moment I commit, I'm already looking for the exit. Just the other day, I even gave up sunbathing, using rain and strong wind as my excuse.

"Then why do you keep trying?" you might ask. Well, every now and then, something like fishing, or Hobonichi, sneaks in there, something I end up loving for a long time, something that actually sticks.

Thank you for coming to Hobonichi today, too. They say someone who quits after three days is a quitter, but you could also argue: at least they lasted three days.
