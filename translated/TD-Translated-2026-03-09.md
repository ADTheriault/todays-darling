## Title: A time machine to the world of 14-year-olds.
## Date: 2026-03-09
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

Even if I tell you about this film, there's no guarantee everyone will be able to see it. If you have the right connections, or good luck, or chase after it earnestly enough, you'll probably manage to see it. Even now, the theaters showing it are limited, but apparently "screenings" are also possible in places that aren't movie theaters. Still, it's not really a loss if you can't see it. There's nothing particularly rare in the film. It's just about thirty-five ordinary eighth graders living their lives, so in a way, it's like you yourself are already there.

That said, these ordinary eighth graders, ordinary as they are, are all incredibly (to use the trendy way of putting it) "precious."

The title is "Bookmark at 14." It's a documentary film that listens to the stories of thirty-five students in one class at one school and films their daily lives. Each of them has their own story, of course, but the film doesn't deliberately add shading or drama to those stories. All thirty-five are covered more or less equally, so even when you want to know more, you can't follow further. There's just those two months or so of their lives until spring comes and they move up a grade. The camera goes to classrooms, commute routes, and sometimes their homes, but there's a comfortable intimacy in the distance it keeps from these fourteen-year-olds.

For each of these fourteen-year-olds, nothing has really begun yet, and nothing has ended either. Every day, like snow piling up, "now" accumulates upon "now."

Naturally, every viewer will find themselves trying to recall what they were like at fourteen. I too, thinking "I might have been even more childish," got on the time machine and spent about two hours looking around, my eyes darting about the world of fourteen-year-olds. The world these thirty-five ordinary kids showed me was, in the best sense, far more old-fashioned and straightforward than the online world we routinely see now. That's right, I felt reassured. "Living as a person" is actually pretty serious, pretty old-fashioned stuff, isn't it.

I'm grateful to the team behind this film and to these fourteen-year-olds. Thank you for coming to Hobonichi again today. Apparently, fourteen is the age when we become biologically capable of creating a "family."
