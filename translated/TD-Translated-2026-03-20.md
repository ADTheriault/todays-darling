## Title: What happens next, I don't know.
## Date: 2026-03-20
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

I remember learning in school that Gutenberg's printing press spread the "book" to the world and delivered "knowledge" to the people. But there's no way everyone could so easily get their hands on books and read them, right? Sure, compared to hand-copied manuscripts, maybe you could print hundreds of copies at a time, but books must have been terribly expensive. And when you really think about it, could ordinary people even read in the first place? It probably took centuries after Gutenberg's invention before the "book" finally came to resemble what we think of as a book today.

Still, once a book was published, there was no changing what was written inside. If you wanted a different idea or a different story, you had to find a different book. Periodicals, newspapers and magazines, were another matter. They didn't rewrite what came before, but they could convey new things as time moved forward. The Japanese word for newspaper literally means "newly heard," after all. Even if the total volume of information wasn't large, it was light and it could change. And thanks to expanding transportation networks, it could reach everyone, quickly and far away. What's more, because periodicals could track the movement of society and report, "This is how things were before, but here's how they are now," that very quality earned them credibility. The ability to correct mistakes became something that mattered.

Then the internet arrived, and once everyone started using it, there was no more typesetting, no printing, no binding, no shipping. Weightless "information" could reach the entire world in an instant, and anyone could create something equivalent to a "book" or a "newspaper." At that point, it was no longer just a matter of things being "changeable." The real question became: where is anything that doesn't change? That became the more important thing to search for. A staggering number of competing "truths" kept vying with one another. Deciding what was "correct" grew harder and harder.

And while all of this was happening, AI came along and spread into our lives, ready to tell us, "This is what is considered correct."

Thank you, as always, for visiting Hobonichi today. Books, newspapers, the internet, AI, the person next to you. They're all right here in front of us.
