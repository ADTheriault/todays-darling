## Title: A merchant's perspective: Akinding.
## Date: 2026-03-18
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

"Go study!" or "All you ever do is play, you never study!" Adults have been saying things like that forever, but I wonder how it really works nowadays. When someone tells you to "go study," what are you actually supposed to do? Kids don't know what that means in concrete terms, so all they can really do is pretend to study. If there's homework or a workbook to get through, at least that feels like something you can point to and call "studying." But without those things, what on earth are you supposed to do? Something like, "Just read your textbook, that's enough," or "Just spend some time remembering what you did at school today, that's enough." Even coming up with ideas like that is a remarkable thing. I think that simply knowing what to do already makes you a kid who can study. Don't you think?

Now, of course, I don't think there's anything wrong with the kid who's told they're "always just playing." I know full well that you can't always tell where play ends and learning begins. We learn how to live through play, after all.

But setting that aside, let me come back to the subject of "go study." Something I say all the time, "Come up with more ideas," is actually very similar to "go study." Except in this case, you're being told to think about things that have no right answer, which makes it even harder to know what to do. Ideally, you'd be churning out ideas constantly, hit or miss, just keeping them coming. But the question is, how do you get to that point?

On this very topic, my old friend Tomoyoshi Akiyama wrote a book called "This Is How Sense Is Born," sharing his own methods. I bought fifty copies through Hobonichi because the book is genuinely useful. We also had him come as a guest to the Hobonichi dojo. It really drives home that you can be picking up material for ideas all the time, without pause.

Personally, I swapped out the word "sense" and replaced it with "chance." In other words, this is also about chances to sell, chances to earn. It means adopting the perspective of a merchant. I even coined a term for it: "akin-ding," from the Japanese word for merchant.

Thank you for visiting Hobonichi again today. "Akin-ding" and "branding" are, come to think of it, somewhat alike.
