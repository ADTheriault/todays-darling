## Title: The joy of cherry blossoms tinged with loneliness.
## Date: 2026-03-17
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

At last, it's drawing near. Yes, spring, when the cherry blossoms bloom. Every year, right around the time Setsubun passes in February, I find myself shivering through each cold day, yet thinking: just a little longer, and I'll forget all about this cold as though it were never real. "Spring cannot be far behind," I remind myself. We know, with certainty, that warmth will come. And as its signal, the cherry blossoms open. The season arrives when everything, everywhere, flushes with color.

The trees along Aoyama Cemetery, bare and black right now, will soon be alive with blossoms. The moat along the Imperial Palace that I pass on my way to work, the tree-lined road leading to the Nippon Budokan, the embankments of Chidorigafuchi, all of it will be steeped in cherry-blossom pink, as if the whole city were celebrating something. Spring will come, almost riotously so.

As I walk, as I drive, I'm already imagining the cherry blossom season I've watched unfold so many times. They haven't bloomed yet, but they will, soon. Spring is coming again. It's going to come whether we're ready or not. And though I genuinely look forward to it while I'm waiting, even before there's the faintest sign of blossoms, my mind races ahead to the moment they've already fallen, and I feel a pang of loneliness. Another year has come full circle, just like that.

Lately, every time I pass through the Daikanmachi area, I've been watching the cherry trees closely. And now, sure enough, the news programs have started talking about bloom forecasts. When will the cherry trees at Yasukuni Shrine open? That day, it seems, is just around the corner. Give it a few more days and they'll be showing footage of those tiny, endearing buds. It's still cold out, and yet the blossoms are about to open. Spring is almost here. I've been looking forward to it, and it makes me a little sad, too.

Last year, the year before, and every year before that, I waited for the cherry blossoms this same way, caught between anticipation and melancholy. And once they bloomed, I'd start thinking about the day they'd scatter, growing sadder still, even as I began looking ahead to summer.

Thank you for visiting Hobonichi again today. Even the waiting isn't all joy. There's a thread of loneliness woven through it, too.
