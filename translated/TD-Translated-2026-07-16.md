## Title: Remembering "creating customers" once again.
## Date: 2026-07-16
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

It was a quarter of a century ago now. I would stuff a bag full of mystery novels and go spend the New Year's holiday on Bali. Poolside, in my room, in the little pavilion out in the garden, for five whole days I did nothing but read. I kept this up for several years running.

But one year, for reasons I no longer recall, a business book by Drucker had found its way in among the mysteries. I'd chosen it and packed it myself, of course, but only in the spirit of "maybe I'll give this a quick look," nothing more.

I started reading it in that same casual mood, in between my usual, familiar mysteries, and wouldn't you know it, the thing turned out to be more interesting than the mysteries. I found myself not even wanting to put it down at mealtimes.

Looking back on it now, I couldn't tell you what exactly I understood, or what I took away from it. These days I can only guess at what I was feeling back then. But there is one line I have always remembered, one I still bring up in conversation.

"There is only one valid definition of business purpose: to create a customer."

There is only one valid definition of business purpose: to create a customer.

"Creating a customer." It stays interesting even if you phrase it a little differently. "Bringing a market into being." "Can you win someone's loyal patronage?" "Can you create a customer who wasn't there before?"

It doesn't have to be some intensely businesslike business, either. You could say the same thing to someone doing comedy. Rather than fighting over customers, you bring customers into existence. And that means a new relationship beginning, and beyond that, it means new sources of enjoyment multiplying.

I used to think of work as something rather dangerous, the sort of thing captured in old sayings like "the moment a man steps outside, he has seven enemies" or "sharp enough to steal the eye from a galloping horse." But "creating a customer," now that sounds like it could be fun.

The times are one long succession of turning points, large and small. Yet however things change, I don't think "creating a customer" ever will. Even with the storm of AI looming right there in front of us, the work remains the same: creating a customer.

Thank you for coming to Hobonichi again today. Even to an amateur like me who knows nothing about management, this felt like something I could truly understand.
