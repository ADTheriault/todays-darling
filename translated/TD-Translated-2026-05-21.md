## Title: It's not really a "culture festival," you know.
## Date: 2026-05-21
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

At last, the Life & Enjoyment Exhibition is almost here. There's a certain tension running through the Hobonichi office. Everyone's shifted up a gear in their own way. Of course, the exhibition is work, but it also feels something like a company trip where you actually work. Then again, put it that way and people might think, "That sounds awful." And sure, a company trip where you're forced to work would be the worst. I can't quite put it right. People often compare it to a school festival, but "school festival" doesn't feel like enough. There's unevenness, sure, and some things are done better than others, but what we're doing really is work. Maybe the closest way to describe it is "a place where we go through something together." The crew here at the office, the people exhibiting, the people who come as visitors, the people who show up to help out part-time: everyone goes through it together.

What exactly are we going through? Well, that's the part I can't put into words. It's one of those feelings that resists the kind of "putting it into language" that's so fashionable these days. Each person feels it, and we exchange it, share it. Then, afterward, everyone keeps talking it over, as memories, as reflections, as words, as methods. That turns into the wisdom of "next time, let's try it this way." And the feeling that next time we can do even more becomes something like hope. When it's over, we share the exhaustion too, and rest. You find yourself itching to talk to people about all of it. Then you go back to your regular post, to work that isn't the exhibition. Plans for the next one start quietly taking shape, and the cycle toward the future begins.

Michael Polanyi's idea of "tacit knowledge" was developed by Professor Nonaka Ikujiro into the SECI model, and what we're doing might be close to that. Though it could also be that we're all just kicking around a ball labeled "I can't explain it well, but it's good," and having fun doing it. We're not running things according to theory, so here we are before the big event, bustling around as usual.

Thank you, as always, for coming to Hobonichi today. I'm getting myself ready to go the full distance through that long stretch, in my own way.
