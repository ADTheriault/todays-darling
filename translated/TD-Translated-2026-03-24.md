## Title: Milk memories, falling like drops.
## Date: 2026-03-24
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

If you asked ChatGPT about the trends in milk consumption in Japan, I'm sure it could give you an answer right away. There probably weren't many cows right after the war, and during the era of rapid economic growth, demand from school lunches must have been high. That kind of data is well documented. But when it comes to what "milk" meant in people's actual lives, I think you're better off asking a real person.

In my own case, we didn't get milk with our school lunches. We drank powdered skim milk supplied by the United States. It was terrible. But at home, there was such a thing as milk delivery. Glass bottles of milk brought to your "milk box" every day. My memories of those milk bottles from that era are pure nostalgia now. I even wrote empty bottles into a song called "Come by Bicycle."

In a drama called "Kizudarake no Tenshi" (Angel with Scars) that started in 1974, there was a popular scene where the main character, played by Hagiwara Kenichi, pulls a paper carton of milk straight from the fridge and chugs it down. The one-liter paper carton of milk was a way of expressing the incredibly basic nutritional intake of young people in that era. It was the "rational choice" of a single-minded young man who couldn't be bothered with meals. The opposite of today's "foodie" hero, and yet that kind of character was the one the audience loved.

Around the start of the 1980s, I had a solo-comedy performer appear at an event I was involved in. It was "Denden," who's now a thoroughly distinguished actor. The opening of his routine left a real impression on me. He'd ask the audience in this exaggerated way, "Hey everybody, are you drinking your milk?" The bit was essentially asking whether the audience was still living that petit-bourgeois lifestyle that was already starting to feel outdated.

On this same idea of what milk represented, RC Succession wrote a lyric in their song "Kimochi E" (Feels Good) that goes, "more than the guys drinking milk." Come to think of it, I believe it was around this time that I appeared in a commercial for milk, alongside the owner of Naniwaya Souhonten, the famous taiyaki shop.

I've just been jotting down memories as they come to me, but this kind of thing doesn't tend to survive as information or data. Thank you, as always, for visiting Hobonichi today. In the world of personal passions and interests, it seems like what endures isn't information but memory.
