## Title: Things about roads.
## Date: 2026-04-13
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

Something occurred to me once. I was gazing out at a wide road, not really thinking about anything, and it hit me: this is really wide. A road with three lanes on each side has six lanes total. Add the sidewalks on both sides, and you're talking about a huge amount of area. You could fit several small buildings in that space. A road, when no one's walking or driving on it, is just empty. Nobody lives there. There are no shops. A road is basically a "line," in geometric terms, and I don't think people normally consider the surface area of a road. Since it's just something you travel back and forth on, you never think about it in terms of size. But in reality, it covers an enormous area.

And apparently, building a road takes a staggering amount of labor, materials, and money. I mean, I've never built a road myself, so I wouldn't really know, but you can imagine, can't you? Even laying down a single meter of road at a reasonable width must be a huge undertaking. Roads in ancient times must have been even harder to build. And whether or not a road exists, whether or not a road has been laid down, matters enormously to human society. Just like securing a water source, roads must have been considered vital since the earliest days. You can't live on a road. You can't build on it. It doesn't produce anything. On top of that, maintaining and protecting the road costs money. A road exists solely for the purpose of going back and forth. And yet that "going back and forth" is life itself.

Think about it. In the human body, blood vessels, thick and thin, are threaded throughout every corner, and when you die, that "back and forth" stops. (I just casually looked it up on ChatGPT, and apparently the total length of blood vessels in the human body is 100,000 kilometers. That's enough to circle the earth two and a half times.)

Roads are often used as metaphors for all kinds of things, but when you stop and think about it, they really are something remarkable. Traveling back and forth on roads that already exist is important, and carving out new roads where none existed before is extraordinary too. I finally understand why every middle-aged guy wants to sing "My Way."

Thank you, as always, for visiting Hobonichi today. When you add the character for "road" or "way" to something, like the way of tea or the way of calligraphy, it becomes a path that anyone can walk.
