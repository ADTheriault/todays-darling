## Title: Musings of a big-sister teacher.
## Date: 2026-03-12
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

This past week, I went to an elementary school in Akagi, then to Kesennuma, and now I'm back in Tokyo. The last thing I did was visit Tsunakan in Karakuwa to see Ichiyo Kanno. We made a fuss catching up, offered prayers facing the sea, and made sure each other was doing well. We both made each other promise to come to the other's funeral. Lately, that's pretty much all I've been doing. "Don't assume your money or your friends will always be around." And I mean it. The slightest shift in circumstances can change your fate entirely. It's fine to trust that tomorrow will come, but the day after, or ten years from now, or forever? You can't count too much on any of that. You've got to live today. Saying mildly ominous things at just the right moment has always been a specialty of mine, so please forgive me.

Ichiyo said she'd always relied on my boss the way you'd rely on a big brother. But when we met yesterday, she'd been promoted: "Kanako-san is like a father." That's something. My boss, my wife, and now a father figure. Meanwhile, apparently I'm "like a big sister," which means I'm the kind of person she can say anything to. We've clearly transcended all social notions of gender here. On top of that, she sometimes accidentally calls me "sensei," so I've ended up as "Big Sister Sensei." Fine. I'll just go ahead and live the rest of my life as Big Sister Sensei.

In Kesennuma, I saw lots of children. Of course, I pay close attention to children in Tokyo too. As I've gotten older, children have started to look radiant to me. When adults are busy cataloging their various despairs, when they're tallying up the pessimism and the darkness day after day, children are the future simply by being there.

On a related note, AI doesn't actually believe in the future. It doesn't believe. It simply assumes, statistically, that there probably will be one, and works from there. Which, in its own way, I find rather amusing.

Thank you for coming to Hobonichi again today. I bumped my ribs on the armrest of a hotel chair, and it kind of hurts.
