## Title: Don't rush to finish.
## Date: 2026-03-21
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

What I keep telling myself, and everyone around me, is this: don't rush toward completion. I say it often at Hobonichi, too. This doesn't mean giving up on completing things. It's that your image of what "complete" looks like is always shifting. If you try to race toward whatever "complete" means at one particular moment, you start wanting to prepare for every contingency, to anticipate every possible scenario, and before you know it, you can't even make your first move. All that piles up are impossible problems, worries, and worst-case fantasies. I have that urge in me too, the impulse to rush toward completion. I always have, and I still do. I think that's exactly why I've written things like this so many times, as a reminder to myself.

Once, I put it this way: "Let's meet on low ground." Communication, I said, should begin at a low, humble place, a point where both sides can give a little. When people announce, "Two ambitious visionaries have joined forces!" it usually turns out to be nothing more than a castle in the air. And that phrase I use, "Put hands and feet on your dreams," isn't that very much in the spirit of not rushing toward completion? Even once a dream has hands and feet, maybe all it can do is take a few steps, carry a light load. You have no idea what good thing might eventually come together from there.

I've heard that when young people set out to do something, the very first thing they do is try to gather sponsors and funding. And of course, in that case you need to present a finished blueprint, and people push you to complete things fast. But it's not just young people. Society as a whole works that way now.

When you keep going without rushing, enjoying the process, both the circumstances around you and you yourself change, and a path toward some new kind of completion starts to come into view. That's because human growth itself works exactly that way.

Thank you for coming to Hobonichi today. Being busy and rushing through things, it really does take the joy out of them, doesn't it?
