## Title: Just seeing a lot of things, and other matters.
## Date: 2026-07-07
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

Punch, the little monkey at the Ichikawa Zoological and Botanical Garden, may well have become a full-fledged international star by now. He's grown up quite a bit, getting closer to what you might call a young man. These days, it seems he even looks after the newer baby monkeys, the ones born not long ago.

Thanks to Punch, I've spent far more time watching the monkeys on their hill than I ever have before. Never in my life have I spent this much time gazing at a monkey enclosure. I think there's something truly wonderful about simply watching, a lot, without studying or trying to learn anything from it.

When it comes to the things we love, we just watch. A lot. Not for any purpose. Just to watch. Because the watching itself feels good. We don't only look from straight on. We look from a distance too. We look from behind, where they can't see us looking back. We watch them when they're still, and when they're moving.

It's not because we want to communicate our love to them. It's entirely one-sided. We watch simply because we like them. Babies and children are watched this way. To varying degrees, lovers probably are too. The people we admire and follow are watched this way. It's purely selfish on the part of the watcher. We watch because it feels good.

Still, some people can't help searching for the reason. "But why do I like this?" If the searching is fun, then by all means, search away. But rather than hunting for reasons, you're better off just doing more watching.

I recently saw a documentary about Takeshi Yoro. When asked about his health, his illness, his thoughts on things, he answered every question carefully, choosing his words with care. But the times he seemed happiest, more than anything, were when he was making specimens of his beloved insects, or when he was with fellow insect enthusiasts. There was obviously no reason behind it, and he didn't seem to have any intention of putting those specimens to practical use.

How wonderful it is to have something you love. That's what a living person really wants to do, isn't it?

The ropes of "why" and "what for" may be binding us hand and foot without our knowing it.

Thank you for coming to Hobonichi again today. Toys exist in this world, and there are people who never stop playing.
