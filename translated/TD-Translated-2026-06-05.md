## Title: A sturdy husband is precious, and "all right" is something to be grateful for.
## Date: 2026-06-05
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

Lately, I've been thinking more and more about how important it is to be "sturdy." In Miyazawa Kenji's poem "Be Not Defeated by the Rain," there's a line about having a sturdy body. The poem seems to contain Kenji's vision of the ideal human being, and "sturdiness" was clearly a big part of that.

"Sturdy" is different from "strong." You can be sturdy without being strong. It's more like being hard to break, not easily falling ill, that sort of thing. If you think in terms of offense and defense, it's the latter. It means you don't fall apart. At first glance, that might not seem like much, but even strong things can be fragile, can break all the time. What I find appealing now is the idea of not being particularly strong, yet still not falling apart.

In "Be Not Defeated by the Rain," the person who lives being called a nobody by everyone, never praised, never a bother to anyone. The fact that this person is specifically described as having a sturdy body feels like something deeply important.

What did "sturdy" originally mean as a word, anyway? It's only now that I've gotten curious enough to look it up. The Japanese word is made of two characters: one meaning a unit of length, and the other meaning "husband" or "grown man." Put them together and, oh, I see. It's a full-grown man of good stature. A fine, fully-fledged man, apparently. So "sturdiness" refers to the qualities such a man would possess. I feel like I understand and don't understand at the same time, but, well, that's fine.

And then a particularly fine man would be "greatly sturdy," which is also the everyday word for "all right" or "no problem." So that's why "all right" means "all right"? Well, okay then. Starting to dig into the etymology actually made the nuance fly right out the window.

But what I originally wanted to say was simply this: I've come to realize, even at this late stage, that being sturdy matters. As long as you stay sturdy, you can probably stay all right.

Thank you for visiting Hobonichi again today. If you can manage to stay sturdy, you'll be ready for whatever comes next.
