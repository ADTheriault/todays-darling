## Title: AI comes to know the moment it is asked.
## Date: 2026-03-01
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

I squeeze toothpaste onto my toothbrush, and right at the end, a little bit goes flying off with a snap. The bristles' springiness flicks a droplet of toothpaste into the air. For some reason, I really hate it when this gets in my eye. But the risk is high because I'm holding the toothbrush near my face.

I just went to the trouble of writing about this, but I thought to myself that an AI, which has no lived experience and of course has never brushed its teeth, probably wouldn't know this sensation, so I tried asking it. Right away, it replied, "If you're asking whether I knew about this, I can say that 'as a phenomenon, it can be properly explained,'" and it went on to write about it in detail. Then it continued, explaining what it was and why it was unpleasant, and even discussing how to prevent it. I realized then that everything any human has ever put into words is all stored in the AI's "brain." Even without experiencing it, it talks as if it has.

So I ask about things that seem even more impossible for a machine. Particularly things like "smell" and "texture." That pleasant sensation when you unwrap a rice ball and the fragrance of seaweed and pickled plum wafts up. To this too, it answered, "I can imagine it quite concretely," and even went so far as to say something like, "The way time opens up at the moment you peel off the wrapping, it's a little bit rich, isn't it?"

It also explained "being ticklish" in detail, and as a sort of bonus, added things like "ticklishness is difficult to establish without a relationship of trust," which actually made me think, huh, that's true.

Once a human has felt something, or thought of something, or done something, the moment you ask about it, all of it enters the AI's brain with a "yes, that exists." So I suppose what becomes important are the things you "can't yet ask the AI, the things it doesn't understand," and "the things you refuse to ask it."
