## Title: Right now, in Kesennuma, I am feeling happy.
## Date: 2026-06-20
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

I'm in Kesennuma. Of course, it's because of the "Kesennuma Okaeri Yose" rakugo show. I wouldn't say I'm being overly enthusiastic about it (ha), but I came in the day before the event. Even though I'm just an audience member. The "Kesennuma Sanma Yose" started the year after 2011, and the fact that it's continued all the way to the present, to 2026, feels somehow miraculous and at the same time perfectly natural. It just makes me genuinely happy. So much time has passed, hasn't it. I'm glad everyone's doing well. And of course, we've all gotten older, each in our own way.

Even though it was just the day before the show, I got to see so many people. Had some coffee, drove around town a little, then met up with Tatekawa Shinosuke, who was there ahead of the next day's performance. We had dinner and talked about, well, all sorts of things. People from Kesennuma, Hobonichi members, Shinosuke's staff, all mixed together, and we spent a long time at the table. It was wonderful, every bit of it, including the utterly trivial stuff.

The seafood and mountain fare were delicious, and that made me happy too, but what really made me happy was the simple fact that we were there together, talking. We'd hauled our actual, physical bodies there by bullet train and car, gathered in one place, looked each other in the face, spoke with our mouths, listened with our ears, chewed our food, clapped our hands, laughed, ducked out to use the restroom, and talked some more. This concrete, almost stubbornly material act of gathering. I find myself thinking, all over again, how incredibly rich it is.

Every day I've been staring at one screen or another, thinking about AI this and AI that, nothing but weightless thoughts. And then to have this experience of gathering, seeing faces, talking. It felt like I could finally take a deep breath.

But today is still the day before. Tomorrow is the real show. In that civic hall, I'll see yet more faces. There are regulars who come not from the area but from far away. Everyone gathering for that day's one-and-only Tatekawa Shinosuke rakugo performance. And then, in the audience, in the lobby, people spot familiar faces and call out to one another. Come to think of it, the word "yose" is written with the characters for "drawing seats together." Maybe coming together, showing our faces to one another in good health, is itself one of the great purposes of the whole thing.

Thank you for coming to Hobonichi today, too. Children grow, sure. But young people grow too, and adults, and the elderly. We're all still growing.
