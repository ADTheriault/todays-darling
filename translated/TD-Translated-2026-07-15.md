## Title: Idle musings that started with a dog's teeth
## Date: 2026-07-15
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

My dog Buiko doesn't mind having her teeth brushed. Maybe she actually hates it, but she puts up with it. So every day she gets a good brushing. And the one doing the brushing, my boss around the house, does it often and does it well.

Even so, plaque builds up, and she still gets cavities and gum disease. Our previous dog, Bouillon, was the same breed, but might have had slightly tougher teeth. If I recall, we did have her plaque removed under general anesthesia, but I think we brushed her teeth a little less often. Buiko gets far better dental care, yet the number of teeth pulled because of cavities or gum disease is higher for her.

Animals don't brush their own teeth. The most they do is chew on something hard now and then. But does that mean they never get cavities? Of course they do. I imagine there are plenty of animals out there with cavities and gum disease.

Long ago, elderly humans lost a lot of teeth too. Dentures were a common joke in manga and the like, but in an era when dentures were a luxury, the teeth must have simply stayed gone.

I haven't asked any AI about this, so I'm just going on instinct, but dinosaurs and all the other animals must have had bad teeth. Although writing it out like that, I'm starting to lose confidence. I suspect they just didn't live long enough for their teeth to fall out.

For creatures in the wild, a single injury or a single illness makes the rest of life hard to survive, so they probably don't make it to the next injury or illness. It seems unlikely there's a pack that keeps nursing a tiger that can no longer move. And when the group moves on to the next place, no one's going to carry it. In the natural world, it's less a case of "one ailment keeps you healthy" and more "one ailment and you're dead."

When you think about it, a dog that lives long enough for its teeth to go bad is, after all, a dog that's part of human society. And for us humans, being able to grumble about our health, "this hurts, that's giving out," is itself proof that we're alive. To be able to live while patching ourselves up as we go, now that's an amazing civilization.

Thank you for coming to Hobonichi again today. Patching up here, papering over there, I'm doing just fine myself.
