## Title: Is the theory of adulthood at 30 too carefree?
## Date: 2026-04-20
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

The world just keeps getting more and more unforgiving. What used to feel like "normal sensibility" has changed so much that it would probably be criticized not as "outdated" but as "way too leisurely." I remember when words like "cost performance" and "time performance" started popping up constantly in everyday conversation, and I thought, "Is that really how things are now?" But maybe we've already reached the point where even questioning the cost-performance, time-performance mindset is itself considered yesterday's news. Then again, staking out new territory by declaring the old way obsolete is something people have been doing since time immemorial.

With that preamble out of the way, I've been thinking about the "adulthood at thirty" idea. It's something I still believe in, and I think others have said similar things. The idea is this: legally, you become an adult at twenty, but in today's world, your twenties are really just an extension of childhood, so wouldn't it make more sense to say that true adulthood begins at thirty? Back then, the word "moratorium" was being thrown around in a negative way. The "adulthood at thirty" idea flipped that on its head and made it a positive thing. Long ago, it was taken for granted that young people would be fully capable workers early on. But society has grown more complex, human lifespans have gotten longer, and isn't it rushing things to decide someone is an adult at twenty?

Speaking from my own experience, I didn't really feel like I'd become an adult until I was around thirty. I've told younger people over and over, "You can still afford to be foolish in your twenties." The ideas and actions of that time, failures and mistakes included, become the foundation for all the years that follow.

And yet, the reality is that the legal age of adulthood has actually been lowered to eighteen. It feels like there's this pressure now: "No time for foolishness." But in an era that just keeps getting more and more unforgiving, isn't the "adulthood at thirty" idea actually more important than ever?
