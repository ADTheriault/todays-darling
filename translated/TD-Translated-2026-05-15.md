## Title: Going out to the mailbox to send a postcard late at night.
## Date: 2026-05-15
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

Before the date could change from May 14th, I walked in my sandals to the nearest mailbox. I wasn't out on some other errand. I was going to mail a postcard. Just minutes earlier, I'd been at the venue for Kenji Ozawa's "Tsuki to Machi no Aidate" concert, and I'd only just gotten home.

"Write anything you want on a postcard and send it to me," he'd said, right at the close of the concert. "As much as possible, today, whatever comes to mind." He said he'd much rather read someone's handwriting on a postcard than neat, easy-to-read fonts on the internet. He went on to explain things like, "If you're in Tokyo, it arrives in a day," and "You don't even need to put a stamp on it, the address is already printed." One after another, these little instructions, all in service of his request: "Please send me a postcard."

The concert was truly wonderful. It was full of colorful schemes and tricks that bordered on mischief, all prepared with a staggering degree of seriousness. The structure of the show, the performances, the singing, all of it was extraordinary in both quality and sheer volume. There's so much I could say about it, and I'm sure someone somewhere will talk about all of it, but what impressed me most of all was the "fierce, tenacious energy" it took to convey everything he wanted to convey. Kenji Ozawa is being born again, right now, all over again. It made me happy to feel like I was sharing in that moment.

But setting all of that aside for now. In the set that had been handed out to everyone in the audience beforehand, there was a postcard and a pencil. Write something and send it, he said. He'd asked so earnestly that I made up my mind to do it. And so the story circles back to where it began.

Reading a postcard might bring joy, but writing one feels good too. Scribbling down in a hurry how wonderful it all was, then half-jogging to the mailbox in the middle of the night, that was a thrill of its own. I imagine hundreds of postcards from people just like me will pile up. Picturing Kenji Ozawa sitting there reading them all, I thought: what an enviable way to end a concert.

Thank you for coming to Hobonichi today, too. Handing out postage-paid postcards to the audience... that's something I'd love to steal someday.
