## Title: "You don't need to know that."
## Date: 2026-06-19
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

Well, all kinds of information comes flooding in, doesn't it. From the sea, from the mountains, from the city, from the people... no, actually, it's mainly from the internet. "How about this?" "Don't you love this?" "This is the best!" It whispers to you, talks at you as though it's all terribly important. There are world crises and industry news you feel you ought to know, weather updates and stories about small animals doing this or that, gossip too lowbrow to bring up in polite company, people hurling their emotions at full volume, funny stories, and an inexhaustible supply of conflicts and squabbles in every size from small to medium. Even when it's total strangers, if a fistfight breaks out in the street, the rubberneckers will gather. And you'd want to stick your head out the window and have a look too.

Without a moment's rest, something is always talking at you. Through some clever information management happening somewhere, the things each person is most likely to want to read come bouncing in, one after another. As long as you're connected to the internet, it never stops. I'm aware that "Hobonichi is one of those things, too." When it comes to how to deal with the internet, I'm right there in the same maze as everyone else, thinking it through.

So yes, I'm obviously still wandering around in that maze. But a few days ago, I had the feeling I'd stumbled on a small trick. What you do is this: right in the middle of reading something, or while you're following one link to the next, you pause and say to yourself, "You don't need to know that." I already knew it. I knew that almost all of it was stuff I didn't need to know. And yet, once the fuse is lit, I can't help chasing after it. With smartphones, there's actually an upside to those annoying ads that are hard to dismiss. They give you a moment to say to yourself, "Hey, you don't need to..." They let you interrupt the inertia, the force of habit, just a little. If you do this consciously a few times, it becomes something you can actually do.

Before resorting to "just throw away your smartphone," try giving yourself a tap on the shoulder. I'd really recommend it.

Thank you for visiting Hobonichi again today. No one has infinite curiosity, so if you decide to stop, you can.
