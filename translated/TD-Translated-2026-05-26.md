## Title: The form of manzai, and fiction.
## Date: 2026-05-26
## Author: Shigesato Itoi
## Content:

The format of manzai comedy holds a real advantage in our current age, and that's because you have a partner right there beside you to play the straight man. This means the one playing the fool can say outrageous things, stupid things, irresponsible things, even dangerous things. Because their teammate is right there to say, "No way!" and pull them back to common sense. Even if one half of the duo says something wrong, the team as a whole can claim it still falls within the rules of society. In a time when criticism and opposing views fly in from every conceivable angle, this may be the most survivable form of expression there is.

If a solo performer stood at the microphone and said the same things, those words would be taken as "his opinion," plain and simple, and sometimes held against him as "his mistake." But when you really think about it, comedic talk was never meant to be a statement of personal views. It was supposed to be a craft, an art of making people laugh. And yet, because it overlaps with the speaker's own voice, people are quick to assume, "So that's what this comedian actually thinks." But if you only ever say things that stay safely within the bounds of common sense, you'll never produce a single laugh. In that sense, the manzai format, with its built-in self-criticism, is perfectly suited to an era like ours.

Writing, however, is not a team act. When you say something on the page, it belongs to you alone. You can't have someone jumping in beside you to correct you as you go. Well, you could try, but writing while preemptively apologizing for every possible objection would be so constrained as to be unbearable. And that, I think, is exactly why fiction is so interesting. Characters in a novel are free to transgress. You can give voice to evil, to hypocrisy, to madness. This is a possibility we ought to open up even further.

Thank you, as always, for visiting Hobonichi today. Come to think of it, isn't it true that so much of what we know about being human, we first learned from novels?
